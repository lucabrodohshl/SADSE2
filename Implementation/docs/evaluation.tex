\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate CRR against three research questions:
\textbf{RQ1 (Efficiency)}---how much does CRR reduce invocations of the expensive
MILP optimizer, relative to full cache revalidation, when the model is refined?
\textbf{RQ2 (Correctness)}---does CRR preserve exact optimality under the evolved
model, and what does the prior no-revalidation approach cost?
\textbf{RQ3 (Sensitivity \& scalability)}---how do the savings vary with
refinement type, footprint size $|\Delta|$, and problem scale?

\subsection{Setup}
We implement CRR on the fleet task-assignment MILP of Section~\ref{sec:motivating}:
$M$ drones cover $J$ tasks under a chosen configuration $k$, minimizing total
fleet energy subject to a per-agent battery-reserve budget
$\mathit{usable}=(1-\rho)\,C/s$. Energy is agent-independent, so the objective is
configuration-determined ($v^\ast=\min_k\sum_j E[k,j]$) while the budget couples
the configuration choice to a bin-packing assignment: tightening it can render the
cheapest configuration infeasible and force a costlier one. A cache is populated
under $\mathcal{M}_0$ with one proven-optimal entry per operating regime (ODD),
modelled as a wind factor scaling energy from \emph{calm} to \emph{strong}; each
entry is enriched with its certificate, dependency set, and validity ranges and
inserted into the reverse index. We then apply the three refinements---Type~II
(tightened battery reserve), Type~III (tighter objective linearization), and
Type~I (a new humidity factor)---and revalidate. We compare against
\emph{full revalidation} (re-solve every entry; the naive $N$-solve upper bound
and optimality ground truth) and \emph{no revalidation} (the stale $\mathcal{M}_0$
cache). All runs are deterministic.

\subsection{RQ1: Efficiency}
On a cache of $N{=}16$ entries, a representative refinement of each type is
revalidated with \emph{zero} MILP re-solves (Table~\ref{tab:crr-efficiency}), a
$100\%$ reduction versus full revalidation, at exact optimality: entries are
discharged through the cheap stages ($6$ certify and $10$ warm-repair for
Type~II; all $16$ certify for Types~III and~I). As severity grows the population
escalates through the cost-ordered stages
(Fig.~\ref{fig:crr-stages}): for Type~II the split moves
$S2/S3/S4 = 16/0/0 \to 6/10/0 \to 4/6/6 \to 1/3/12$ as the reserve tightens, and
the re-solve reduction degrades gracefully from $100\%$ to $25\%$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:crr-reduction})---CRR invokes the optimizer only for entries whose
integer optimum genuinely changed, and never does worse than full revalidation.
Type~III holds at $100\%$ across the whole range: a tighter linearization
preserves the configuration ranking, so every optimum is corrected by an
arithmetic certificate or a warm repair, never a re-solve.

\subsection{RQ2: Correctness}
Across all $15$ refinement points (three types $\times$ five severities) CRR is
\emph{sound}: the maximum optimality gap against the full-revalidation ground
truth is $7.1\times10^{-15}$ (numerically zero), empirically confirming
Theorem~\ref{thm:soundness} at $\tau_{\mathrm{req}}{=}0$. The prior
no-revalidation approach is unsafe under model evolution: the stale cache leaves
up to $94\%$ of entries \emph{infeasible} under the tightened Type-II constraint
(and up to $56\%$ under Type~III)---cached plans that now violate the safety
reserve. The dominant cost of staleness is constraint violation, precisely the
safety-relevant error CRR eliminates without a full re-solve.

\subsection{RQ3: Sensitivity and scalability}
A regime-scoped Type-II refinement touching $k$ of $16$ regimes is served by the
reverse index: entries outside the footprint are reused at Stage~1 without
examination, so the number examined tracks $|\Delta|$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:crr-footprint}), while the number of \emph{expensive} re-solves
saturates at $3$ even when $|\Delta|$ touches all $16$ entries---only the few
regimes whose optimum migrates ever reach a full solve. CRR's advantage grows
with scale: the speedup over full revalidation rises from $1.0\times$ ($M{=}3$)
to $91\times$ ($M{=}12$) as individual solves become costlier
(Fig.~\ref{fig:crr-scale}), and it sustains a $100\%$ re-solve reduction with
$6$--$13\times$ speedup as the cache grows to $N{=}32$.

\subsection{Threats to validity}
CRR runs against the real task-assignment MILP (PuLP/CBC); every solver call is
counted and typed (none / arithmetic / LP-class repair / full MILP). As PuLP
exposes no true warm dual-simplex, Stage~3 is realized as a fixed-configuration
re-assignment---an honest ``cheap, no-branching'' proxy exploring one
configuration rather than all $K$; the stage logic and the headline reduction are
exact. The study is a controlled instantiation of the running example: absolute
speedups depend on solver and instance size, but the stage distribution, the
soundness result, and the footprint/scale trends are properties of the mechanism.
